# Chapter 15 — Receiving and responding to an offer

The interview went well. The recruiter came back with good news. There's now an offer waiting in your dashboard. This is the part of the journey people usually rush — they're excited, they're afraid of losing the offer, they say yes too quickly. Don't.

This chapter walks through reviewing an offer carefully, the three things you can do with it (accept, negotiate, decline), and when each is the right call. The next chapter (Chapter 16) goes into the negotiation tooling in detail.

## Where the offer appears

When an employer creates an offer, you see it in two places:

- **My Applications** — the application's status moves to `Offer`
- **Inbox** — usually with a notification message from the recruiter or hiring manager

Open the application and you'll see the offer details. The platform's offer system tracks offers as a structured record — not just a PDF letter — so you can see and compare each component.

## What's actually in an offer

A typical offer has more parts than people realise. Read every line:

| Component | Why it matters |
|-----------|----------------|
| **Base salary** | The headline number. Don't stop here. |
| **Bonus structure** | Annual bonus, target percentage, performance triggers. Often the difference between two offers. |
| **Signing bonus** | One-off cash incentive for joining. Common at senior levels and in competitive markets. |
| **Equity / shares** | Where applicable. Vesting schedules vary wildly — read the small print. |
| **Benefits** | Medical aid, retirement contribution, life insurance, leave policy, training budget. Real money. |
| **Start date** | Negotiable in almost all cases. |
| **Notice period** | What you owe them when you eventually leave. Don't accept punishing notice clauses without thinking. |
| **Probation period** | Standard in SA. Length and terms vary. |
| **Reporting line** | Who you report to. Sometimes named, sometimes vague. Worth confirming. |
| **Job title** | Make sure it matches what you interviewed for. Title creep on offer letters happens. |
| **Location** | Office, hybrid, or remote. Get specifics, not just "flexible". |
| **Working hours** | Standard, shift, on-call expectations. |
| **Conditions** | Things you must satisfy before the offer is firm — references, background checks, qualifications. |

## Step 1 — Read the whole offer slowly

The first time you open an offer, read all of it. Do not skim. Do not respond yet. Do not message anyone yet.

Make a copy of the key numbers somewhere (Application Notes is a good place). You're going to compare them against:

- **Your current package** (if you have one)
- **Market rates** for the role (Salary Insights — see Chapter 16)
- **Other live offers** (if you're in the lucky position of having more than one)

## Step 2 — Sleep on it

Almost every offer feels different the next morning. The excitement of the win is always loudest in the first hour. By the next day you can think more clearly.

Most employers will not pull an offer because you took 24 hours to consider it. The ones that would, you don't want to work for.

You can usually tell the recruiter or hiring manager:

> "Thank you so much — I'm really excited. Can I take 24–48 hours to review the details and come back to you?"

That's normal. It's expected. Use the time.

## Step 3 — Compare against the market

Open **Salary Insights** (`/dashboard/individual/salary-insights`) and look up the role + experience level + location. The platform shows market salary data — typical ranges, percentile breakdowns, and how this offer compares.

This is where you find out whether the offer is generous, market-standard, or underpaying you. Without this comparison, you're negotiating in the dark.

If the base salary is at or above the 75th percentile for your role and location, you're being treated well. If it's at the 25th percentile or below, there's room to negotiate (or to walk).

## Step 4 — Decide which of three responses to give

You have three options:

### Option A — Accept

Accept if all of the following are true:

- The package meets or exceeds what you'd expected and what the market suggests is fair
- You're confident about the role, the team, and the company
- You have no other live processes you'd want to wait for
- The conditions and small print are acceptable

**To accept**, respond through the Inbox with a clear acceptance message. The platform's offer status will move to `accepted`. Your application moves to `Hired`.

A good acceptance message is short:

> "Hi [name], thank you for the offer. I'm pleased to formally accept and look forward to joining [company] on [date]. Please let me know what next steps you need from me."

That's it. Don't apologise for taking time. Don't over-thank. Don't add caveats.

### Option B — Negotiate

Negotiate if:

- The base salary is below market for the role
- A specific component (bonus, leave, signing bonus) is significantly worse than expected
- You have a competing offer and want to give this employer a chance to match
- There are practical conditions you need adjusted (start date, location, working pattern)

**Don't negotiate just for the sake of it.** Negotiation is a real conversation, not a formality. If the offer is genuinely fair and you're going to accept either way, just accept.

If you're negotiating, Chapter 16 walks through how to use the **Salary Negotiator** tool to build a real case rather than just ask for more.

### Option C — Decline

Decline if:

- The package is significantly below what you need
- Something about the role, team, or company has shifted since you interviewed
- You've accepted another offer
- Your circumstances have changed (you're no longer looking, you got a counter-offer from your current employer, etc.)

**To decline**, respond through the Inbox with a polite, brief, non-apologetic message:

> "Hi [name], thank you very much for the offer. After careful consideration, I've decided not to move forward at this time. I really appreciated the conversations with the team and wish [company] the best. Best regards, [your name]."

Reasons to *not* over-explain:

- You don't owe a detailed reason. "Decided not to move forward" is a complete sentence.
- Detailed reasons sometimes lead to attempts to renegotiate that you don't want
- Leaving the relationship clean preserves the option of working with this company in future

The platform's offer status will move to `declined`. The recruiter sees the response and the file is closed.

## Offer expiry — don't ignore it

Most offers have a response deadline. The platform will show it on the offer record, and the system can also flag escalation triggers internally on the employer's side. If you let an offer expire without responding, it auto-moves to `expired` and the role typically goes to the next candidate.

If you need more time than the deadline allows, **ask for an extension** before it expires. Most reasonable employers will grant a few extra days. Disappearing and missing the deadline is the worst possible move.

## Multiple offers

If you have more than one offer running, you have leverage and you have a problem.

The leverage: you can use one to genuinely improve the other.
The problem: you have to time them carefully, because the slowest one might force a decision on the fastest one.

A few principles:

- **Be honest about what you have.** Don't invent offers. Recruiters talk to each other and to former colleagues; the cost of being caught is permanent.
- **Communicate honestly with both employers.** "I have another live process and need to make a decision by [date]" is fair and standard.
- **Don't accept one and then renege when the other comes through.** Reneging is a real reputational hit, especially in tight industries.
- **Use Salary Insights to anchor both conversations** — that way you're negotiating against the market, not against the other offer.

## What happens after you accept

Acceptance is not the end of the platform's involvement. After acceptance:

- The application moves to `Hired`
- You may be asked for additional documents (qualifications, ID, references) via the Application Portal (Chapter 12)
- The employer may send onboarding information through the Inbox
- The platform's offer record stays in your history for reference

You can also leave a post-interview experience survey if the platform invites you to. These are anonymous and shape future processes — worth filling in.

## Checklist

- [ ] Reviewed the offer details against your expectations
- [ ] Slept on it before responding
- [ ] Checked the salary against Salary Insights
- [ ] Decided consciously whether to accept, negotiate, or decline
- [ ] Responded through the Inbox in writing (not just verbally)
- [ ] If you accepted, you've started thinking about onboarding
- [ ] If you declined, you did so politely and without burning the bridge
