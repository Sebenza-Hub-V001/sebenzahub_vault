# Chapter 23 — Community and mentorship

The hiring path in this manual treats Sebenza Hub as a tool you use mostly alone — a profile, a tracker, a search engine, a coach. The Community and Mentorship features step outside that frame. They put you in contact with other people: other job seekers, people who've already done what you want to do, and people learning the same things you are.

This is the last chapter of the manual. Treat the Community as something you opt into when it suits you, not as another box to tick.

## What's in the Community section

The Individual dashboard has six community pages:

| Page | What it is |
|------|------------|
| **Community Forum** (`/community`) | Open discussion forum with other job seekers |
| **Trending** (`/community-trending`) | Popular topics and active threads |
| **Leaderboard** (`/community-leaderboard`) | User rankings based on participation, badges, contributions |
| **Interview Vault** (`/community-interviews`) | Shared interview experiences and questions, by company and role |
| **Mentor Connect** (`/community-mentors`) | Find and connect with mentors |
| **Badges** (`/community-badges`) | Achievement badges and recognition |

You don't need all of them. You probably need two: **Interview Vault** and **Mentor Connect**.

## The two community features that actually help your job hunt

### Interview Vault

**Interview Vault** (`/dashboard/individual/community-interviews`) is a collection of interview experiences shared by other Sebenza Hub users. Search by company, role, or interview type. You'll find:

- Real questions people were asked
- Format details (number of rounds, panel composition, technical vs behavioural mix)
- How the candidate responded
- The outcome
- Tips for the next person

This is gold when you have an interview booked at a specific company. Even one or two posts about how a company runs its process can change how you prepare.

How to use it well:

1. **Search for the company you're interviewing with** before each interview
2. **Read every entry**, even the older ones — patterns matter more than individual data points
3. **Cross-check against Company Research** (Chapter 14) — Interview Vault tells you the *process*; Company Research tells you the *context*
4. **Contribute back after your own interview**, regardless of the outcome. The vault only works because people share. Sharing your bad interviews is at least as valuable as sharing your good ones.

### Mentor Connect

**Mentor Connect** (`/dashboard/individual/community-mentors`) is the platform's mentorship matching system. You can browse experienced professionals who have opted in to mentor others, filtered by industry, role, seniority, or specific topic.

A good mentor relationship is one of the highest-leverage things in any career. A bad one wastes everyone's time. The difference is almost entirely in how you approach it.

#### Finding a mentor

Filter for someone who:

- Has done the role you want, recently — within the last 5–10 years, ideally
- Is in your sector or has crossed into it from a related sector
- Has been a mentor before (the platform usually shows badges or ratings)
- Is geographically and culturally close enough that their advice transfers

Don't just pick the most senior person available. A senior executive with 30 years of experience may be too far removed from your day-to-day to give relevant advice. A mid-career person five years ahead of you is often more useful.

#### Asking for a mentor relationship

When you reach out, send a short message that includes:

- Who you are professionally (one sentence)
- Why you specifically picked them (one sentence — and make it specific to them, not a generic "I admire your career")
- What you're hoping to get out of the relationship (one sentence)
- What you're prepared to commit to (one sentence — usually one call a month, or one short message a week)

Don't ask for "advice on my career." That's too vague to act on. Ask for help with one specific thing: "I'm trying to decide between continuing in IC roles vs moving to people management — would you be willing to talk through that with me?"

#### Making the relationship work

Good mentees:

- **Show up prepared.** They have a specific question or topic for each conversation.
- **Take notes and act on them.** They come back to the next conversation having done something with the previous one.
- **Don't waste the mentor's time on things they could Google.** They use the conversation for things only a human can give.
- **Are honest about what isn't working.** Including in the mentorship itself.
- **Eventually become mentors themselves.**

Bad mentees:

- Vent more than they ask
- Want validation, not honest feedback
- Don't follow through
- Treat the mentor as a contact to mine for referrals (which usually backfires)

## The features you can mostly ignore

These are present, may be useful for some people, but are not load-bearing for your job hunt:

- **Community Forum and Trending** — fine for occasional context, can be a time sink
- **Leaderboard** — gamification, mostly motivational
- **Badges** — recognition, mostly motivational
- **Peer Teaching** (technically a Learning page, not Community) — useful if you're stuck on a learning goal, niche otherwise

You can use any of these if they happen to suit how you work. None of them is a should-do.

## When community helps and when it doesn't

| Helps when… | Doesn't help when… |
|-------------|---------------------|
| You feel isolated and need to know others are in the same situation | You're using community as a procrastination from actually applying |
| You have a specific question that's hard to Google | You're scrolling forums looking for "the right answer" |
| You want to share what worked for you and help someone else | You're posting to be seen rather than to contribute |
| You're researching a specific company and Interview Vault has data on it | You're reading dozens of generic threads about job hunting in general |

The honest test: did the time you spent in the community section translate into a specific action you took on your own job hunt? If not, it was entertainment, not work.

## A final word

You've reached the end of the manual. If you've worked through Parts 1–4 and reached out to a mentor or contributed to Interview Vault, you've done substantially more than most users. The platform has a lot of features, and most people only use a fraction of them — that's fine. The point was never to use everything; it was to use the right things at the right time.

A few things to take with you:

- **Job hunting is a project, not a lottery.** It rewards structure, attention, and follow-through more than luck.
- **Quality of attention beats volume of effort.** Five thoughtful applications outperform fifty rushed ones, every time.
- **The platform is your ally, not your boss.** Use the tools that work for you. Skip the ones that don't.
- **Diagnose before you act.** Most problems on the platform are misdiagnosed — Part 5 exists for exactly this reason.
- **Stay sustainable.** A six-month job hunt run at a manageable pace beats a six-week sprint that burns you out.

When you land the role, come back and contribute. Add an Interview Vault entry. Become a mentor when you have something to teach. Pay the next person's job hunt forward.

Good luck.
