# Chapter 22 — Market Radar

Market Radar is a passive-monitoring feature. Instead of you going out to look at the market, the platform watches it on your behalf and tells you when something specific you care about happens. It's the most useful feature for people who *aren't* actively job-hunting — and surprisingly useful for active hunters who want to keep an eye on niches they don't want to forget about.

Market Radar lives in your `individualSettings`. It's not a main dashboard page; it's a configuration that quietly produces alerts.

## What you can watch

Market Radar lets you set watches on specific things in the job market:

| Watch type | What it tracks |
|------------|----------------|
| **Companies** | Specific employers — when they post new roles, hire, expand, or change leadership |
| **Roles / titles** | Specific job titles — when new ones appear in your area |
| **Industries** | Whole sectors — when there's significant hiring movement |
| **Skills** | When demand for a specific skill rises or falls |
| **Locations** | Specific cities or regions where role volume changes |

Each watch can be configured with its own threshold (how significant the change has to be before you're alerted) and frequency (how often you want updates).

## Why it matters

Most job hunting is reactive: you decide you need to look, you go look, and you discover a market that's been moving for months without you. Market Radar changes that — by the time you decide to look, the alerts have already been quietly accumulating, and you have months of data on which companies are hiring, which skills are spiking, and which roles are appearing.

Three real use cases:

### Use case 1 — You're employed and not looking, but want to stay informed

Set Market Radar watches on:

- **2–3 specific companies** you'd consider working for
- **Your current role title** (so you see what the market is paying)
- **One or two adjacent skills** you might develop

Frequency: weekly digest. Volume: low. You're not job hunting — you're staying informed.

The first time one of your watch-companies posts a role you'd genuinely take, you have a head start over everyone who only looked when they decided to look.

### Use case 2 — You're actively looking but want to keep niche options alive

You're applying for one main type of role, but there's a secondary niche you'd jump on if it appeared. Set a Market Radar watch for that niche and forget about it. Don't waste daily attention scrolling for it — let the radar catch it.

Frequency: daily or real-time, depending on how rare the role is. Use this sparingly — most things should not be real-time.

### Use case 3 — You're researching the market for a future move

You're not moving for 12 months, but you want to understand where to position yourself. Set Market Radar watches on:

- **The skills your target role wants** (to see whether demand is rising or declining)
- **The companies in that space** (to see who's growing)
- **Salary signals for the role**

You'll have a year of data when you actually start looking, and that data will shape better decisions than a panic search would.

## How to set it up

Open **Settings** → **Market Radar**. For each watch:

1. **Pick the type** — company, role, industry, skill, or location
2. **Specify the target** — the company name, the role title, etc.
3. **Set the threshold** — how big the change has to be to trigger an alert (where applicable)
4. **Set the frequency** — real-time, daily, weekly, monthly
5. **Pick the channel** — email, WhatsApp, in-app

Save. Repeat for each watch.

## Don't over-watch

The temptation with Market Radar is to set 20 watches and feel comprehensive. Don't. Two principles:

1. **Three to five watches is the sweet spot.** More than that and you stop reading the alerts.
2. **Match frequency to importance.** Real-time alerts only for things that genuinely need immediate attention. Weekly digests for everything else.

If your alert volume is more than you'd reasonably read in five minutes a week, you've over-watched. Trim.

## Reviewing what the radar surfaces

Once a week (or whenever your digest lands), spend five minutes on it:

- **Read the alerts.** Don't just glance.
- **Note what's interesting.** A company you didn't know was hiring, a skill that's spiking, a salary trend you didn't expect.
- **Take action only if something is genuinely urgent.** Most weeks the right action is "nothing — but I now know more than I did".
- **Periodically tune.** If a watch keeps producing irrelevant alerts, tighten its threshold or change its target.

## Market Radar vs Saved Searches

These two features overlap. The difference:

| Feature | Best for | What it watches |
|---------|----------|------------------|
| **Saved Search** (Chapter 10) | Active job hunters | New job listings matching your criteria |
| **Market Radar** | Passive observers, future planners | Broader signals — companies, hiring trends, salary movement, skill demand |

If you're actively applying, Saved Searches are your primary tool. If you're not, Market Radar is. They can coexist — Saved Searches for "is anything new I should apply to?" and Market Radar for "is the world I might want to enter changing?"

## When to turn Market Radar off

- When you've taken a new job and need to step away from the platform completely
- When the volume is overwhelming you (often a sign of over-watching, but sometimes a sign that this isn't the right tool for your situation)
- When you've finished researching the market and you're ready to actually apply

It can always be turned back on. Don't treat the configuration as permanent.

## Checklist

- [ ] You know which use case applies to you (employed/looking/researching)
- [ ] Set 3–5 watches, no more
- [ ] Each watch has an appropriate frequency
- [ ] You have a weekly review ritual for the alerts
- [ ] You're prepared to tune watches that produce noise
