# Chapter 16 — Salary Negotiator

You've decided to negotiate the offer. This chapter is about doing that *well*: building a real case grounded in market data, using the AI tools to prepare specific talking points, and handling the conversation without burning the relationship.

Two tools matter here. **Salary Insights** gives you the data. **Salary Negotiator** turns the data into a negotiation strategy. Use both, in that order.

## Two tools, two purposes

| Tool | What it gives you | When to open it |
|------|---------------------|------------------|
| **Salary Insights** (`/dashboard/individual/salary-insights`) | Market salary data — typical ranges, percentiles, trends, comparable roles | First, before deciding what to ask for |
| **Salary Negotiator** (`/dashboard/individual/salary-negotiator`) | AI guidance on the actual negotiation: talking points, scripts, counter-offer structure | Second, once you know the numbers |

If you skip Salary Insights and go straight to the Negotiator, you'll be negotiating without data. If you skip the Negotiator and just rely on Insights, you'll have data but no plan for the conversation. Use both.

> **Open question:** Salary Negotiator may consume credits or require a paid plan depending on your subscription. See [[08-questions/subscription-plans]].

## Step 1 — Open Salary Insights and find your number

Open **Salary Insights** and search for the role + experience level + location combination that matches the offer. The platform shows:

- **Median salary** for the role
- **25th and 75th percentile** ranges
- **How the offer compares** to that range
- **Trend over time** — is the role getting paid more or less than it was?
- **Comparable roles** — useful if your title is unusual or if the market uses a slightly different name

Write down three numbers:

1. **Floor** — the lowest you'd accept. Below this, decline.
2. **Target** — what you actually want. This is what you'll ask for.
3. **Ceiling** — the upper end of plausibility. Above this you start sounding unrealistic.

Your floor should be at least at the 25th percentile for your role and experience. Your target should be near the median or slightly above. Your ceiling should be at the 75th percentile or above only if you have a strong reason (a competing offer, an unusually rare skill, or evidence the role is paid above market at this specific company).

## Step 2 — Open Salary Negotiator

Now go to **Salary Negotiator**. The tool typically asks you to input the offer details (base, bonus, benefits) and your target. It generates negotiation talking points specific to your situation.

What you'll get:

- **Talking points** — specific reasons to ask for more, framed around your strengths and market data
- **Scripted phrases** for common moments (asking for an increase, responding to a "no", trading non-cash items for cash)
- **Counter-offer structure** — what to ask for, in what order, and what to be willing to give up
- **Cautions** — when *not* to push (e.g. when you've already been told the offer is final, or when you don't have leverage)

### Reading the talking points critically

The AI doesn't know your situation in full. Some of its suggestions will be on-target; others will be generic. Treat the talking points as a *menu*, not a script. Pick the two or three that fit you best and discard the rest.

The strongest talking points are the ones that:

- Reference a specific number from Salary Insights ("the market median for this role is X")
- Reference something you actually said in the interview ("you mentioned the team needs someone who can do Y, which is exactly what I've done at Z")
- Reference a concrete commitment you bring ("I can start two weeks earlier than the date in the offer if that's helpful")

The weakest talking points are the ones that just say "I deserve more". Skip those.

## Step 3 — Decide what to ask for

You have more levers than just base salary. Negotiate the package, not just the headline:

| Lever | When it's negotiable | Trade-offs |
|-------|----------------------|------------|
| **Base salary** | Almost always negotiable, but with limits | Easiest to ask for, easiest to be refused |
| **Signing bonus** | Often negotiable when base salary is at the cap of the band | One-off, but solves a specific gap |
| **Annual bonus / target %** | Sometimes negotiable | Less certain than base, but can move the headline figure significantly |
| **Equity** | Negotiable in startups and scale-ups | Highly variable in real value |
| **Start date** | Almost always negotiable | Doesn't change pay but solves real-life problems |
| **Leave allocation** | Sometimes negotiable | Worth real money in tax-free time |
| **Notice period** | Sometimes negotiable | Affects future flexibility |
| **Title** | Sometimes negotiable | Matters more than people think for the next move |
| **Reporting line** | Rarely negotiable in writing | But can be clarified |
| **Remote / hybrid arrangement** | Sometimes negotiable | Real value, especially in SA traffic |
| **Training / development budget** | Often negotiable | Cheap for the employer, valuable for you |

A useful negotiating approach: ask for the base salary increase you want, *plus* one or two non-cash items. The non-cash items give the employer somewhere to land if they can't hit your salary number, and they often agree to some of them when they say no to others.

## Step 4 — Have the conversation

Negotiate in writing, through the Inbox, unless the recruiter or hiring manager has explicitly invited a phone call. Written negotiation is calmer, easier to re-read, and creates a record everyone can refer back to.

A reasonable opening message looks something like this:

> "Hi [name], thank you again for the offer — I'm genuinely excited about the role and the team. After reviewing the package against the market for [role] at this experience level, I wanted to ask whether there's flexibility on a few items:
>
> 1. The base salary is at [their offer]. Based on Salary Insights and the responsibilities of the role, I was hoping for something closer to [your target].
> 2. [Optional non-cash item, e.g. start date, leave, training budget].
>
> I'm fully committed to joining and want to find something that works for both of us. Happy to discuss on a call if that's easier."

That message:

- Opens warm
- Anchors on data, not on personal need
- Gives a specific number rather than "more"
- Bundles a secondary item to give them a fallback
- Ends with explicit commitment
- Offers a call so they don't feel cornered into a written reply

### Things to avoid

- **Don't apologise** for negotiating. It's normal and expected.
- **Don't bluff offers you don't have.** If you do have another offer, mentioning it is fine; if you don't, inventing one is risky.
- **Don't use personal need as a reason** ("I have a bond / a kid / school fees"). Employers pay for value, not need.
- **Don't go silent during the conversation.** Once it's started, respond to messages within 24 hours.
- **Don't escalate emotionally.** Negotiation that gets personal almost never ends well.
- **Don't accept verbally and then try to renegotiate.** Once you've said yes, the negotiation is over.

## Step 5 — Rehearse

If you've been invited to a call, rehearse the conversation once before it happens. The Salary Negotiator can simulate this — feed it your scenario and have it play the recruiter. Run through the most awkward parts:

- "I'm sorry, that number isn't possible."
- "Why do you think you're worth more than what we offered?"
- "Is salary your only concern?"
- "What number would you actually accept?"

Practice answers for each. They don't need to be polished — they need to be calm. The Interview Simulator from Chapter 13 is built for this kind of prep too.

## What to do if they say no

The employer might come back and say the salary is firm. Your options:

1. **Accept anyway**, having been clear that you tried. This is fine and common. You've not damaged the relationship by asking — you've damaged it only if you push back after a clear "no".
2. **Accept the base but get one of the non-cash items.** Often available even when base is fixed.
3. **Decline.** Only if the gap is real and you have realistic alternatives. Don't bluff — if you say "I have to decline" you need to actually be willing to walk.

## What to do if they say yes

If they accept some or all of your asks, get the new terms in writing through the Inbox before you formally accept. The platform will update the offer record with the negotiated terms. Don't accept on a verbal promise.

When everything is settled, accept formally using the steps in Chapter 15.

## A note on negotiation in South Africa

The South African market is generally more conservative on salary negotiation than, say, the US tech market. That doesn't mean you can't negotiate — you absolutely can — but the cultural expectation is that the conversation is calm, evidence-based, and not adversarial. The approach in this chapter is calibrated for that.

For senior roles, executive roles, and roles in international or high-growth tech companies, the negotiation can be more assertive. Read the room based on the conversations you've had during interviewing.
