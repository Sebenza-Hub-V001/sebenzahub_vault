# Chapter 9 — Browse vs Auto Match vs Manual Search

Sebenza Hub gives you three ways to find jobs. Each one suits a different mode of looking. You'll probably use all three over time, but the trick is knowing which to reach for in each situation — otherwise you waste effort and miss roles you would have liked.

What follows is short: the three modes, when each is best, and how to read the signals (match scores, ghost-risk scores) that show up alongside listings.

## The three modes

| Mode | Route | Best for |
|------|-------|----------|
| **All Jobs** | `/dashboard/individual/all-jobs` | Browsing the full market, getting a sense of what's out there |
| **Auto Match** | `/dashboard/individual/auto-match` | Letting AI surface roles that fit your profile |
| **Manual Search** | `/dashboard/individual/manual-search` | When you have specific criteria in mind (salary, location, remote, industry) |

Use them roughly like this:

- **Just starting?** Open All Jobs first to calibrate — see how many roles exist in your space and what they look like. Don't apply to anything yet.
- **Profile complete?** Move to Auto Match. With a strong profile from Part 2, this is where the highest-quality matches will appear.
- **Looking for something specific?** Manual Search. This is the precision tool — use it when you know exactly what you want.

## All Jobs

This is the full job listing on the platform — both jobs **posted directly** by Recruiters and Businesses on Sebenza Hub *and* jobs **aggregated externally** from sources like Adzuna and CareerJet. The mix is important to understand, because the two types behave differently:

- **Posted jobs** are real Sebenza Hub listings. You can apply through the platform, the employer is on Sebenza Hub, your application is tracked end-to-end, and the AI match score is calculated against the actual job spec.
- **Aggregated jobs** are pulled from external sites. Applying may take you off-platform. Tracking is weaker. Match scores may be less precise because the aggregator's job description is what the AI is reading, not always a complete spec.

Both types have their place. Aggregated jobs roughly double the volume you can see, which matters in thin markets. Posted jobs are easier to apply to and easier to track.

### When to use All Jobs

- The first time you log in, just to look around
- When you suspect Auto Match is being too narrow
- When you want to discover companies you didn't know existed

### What to ignore in All Jobs

- Don't apply scattershot. The volume here is overwhelming if you treat every listing as a candidate. Use Favourites (Chapter 10) to triage.
- Don't trust every aggregated listing. Some are stale, some are reposts, some are ghost jobs — the platform flags these via ghost-risk scores (more below).

## Auto Match

Auto Match is the AI feature that scans new jobs against your profile and surfaces the ones it thinks fit. It runs continuously in the background — every time a new job is posted or aggregated, it gets scored against you.

What it uses to match you:

- Your **skills** and proficiency levels (Chapter 7)
- Your **work history** and seniority
- Your **location and remote preference**
- Your **target job title and experience level** from onboarding
- Your **CV** content
- Your **Career DNA** and **Opportunity Weights** (Chapter 21, if you've set them)

What you get back:

- A ranked list of jobs, highest match first
- A **match score** (0–100) per job
- A list of **matched skills** (what you have that the job wants) and **missing skills** (what you lack)

### Why your profile quality matters here

Auto Match is only as good as the input. If your profile is thin, your matches will be vague. If your skills list is over-rated, your matches will be wrong. **Most "Auto Match isn't useful" complaints are actually profile-quality complaints.** Go back to Part 2 if your matches feel off.

### When to use Auto Match

- Daily or weekly check-in once your profile is solid
- As your *primary* discovery channel, with All Jobs and Manual Search as supplements
- After any major profile update (new skill, new credential, new experience)

## Manual Search

Manual Search is the precision tool. You set the filters yourself instead of letting AI infer them.

Available filters typically include:

- **Job title and keywords**
- **Location** (province, city, radius)
- **Remote / hybrid / on-site**
- **Salary range**
- **Industry / sub-industry**
- **Employment type** (permanent, contract, temporary)
- **Experience level**
- **Company name** (if you're targeting specific employers)
- **Posted within** (last 24 hours, last week, last month)

### When to use Manual Search

- You know exactly the role, location, or company you want
- You're filtering by something Auto Match doesn't weight as heavily as you do (e.g. salary floor)
- You're testing the market for a specific niche
- You want to see *all* matching jobs, not just the AI's top picks

### A common mistake

Don't replicate Auto Match by hand. If your filters end up vaguely matching your profile, you're better off just using Auto Match — the AI will do a better job of weighing trade-offs than a flat filter list. Use Manual Search when you genuinely have a specific criterion that's non-negotiable.

## Reading the scores

Two scores show up next to many listings. Understand them before you use them.

### AI Match Score (0–100)

This is how well the platform thinks the job matches *you* specifically. It's based on the same inputs Auto Match uses — skills, history, location, preferences — and it's automatically calculated when a job is posted or when you apply to one.

Rough interpretation:

| Score | What it means |
|-------|---------------|
| **80+** | Strong fit. Apply. |
| **60–79** | Good fit. Worth a closer look — read the JD, decide for yourself. |
| **40–59** | Borderline. Apply only if there's a specific reason it appeals to you. |
| **<40** | Weak fit on paper. The AI thinks you're missing major requirements. |

A low score isn't always a no. The AI doesn't know your context — that you're willing to change industry, that you have a referral, that you're prepared to take a step back. But a low score is a signal worth taking seriously, especially when you're choosing where to spend your effort.

### Ghost Risk Score (0–100)

This is the platform's estimate that the listing is **fake, stale, or otherwise not a real opportunity**. Common reasons jobs get flagged:

- The same job has been reposted month after month with no hires
- The employer doesn't engage with applications
- Aggregator sources show the role has been listed for an unusually long time
- Patterns matching known scam or fishing listings

| Score | What it means |
|-------|---------------|
| **<25** | Looks legitimate. |
| **25–49** | Some risk. Read the listing carefully, look up the employer separately. |
| **50–74** | High risk. Apply only if you have independent reason to believe it's real. |
| **75+** | Very likely a ghost or fraudulent listing. Avoid. |

### Matched skills vs missing skills

Each application also gets two lists:

- **Matched skills** — skills you have that the job wants. These are your strongest pitch points in a cover letter.
- **Missing skills** — skills the job wants that you don't have. These tell you what to address (in the cover letter, in the interview, or by learning — see Chapter 19).

Pay attention to missing skills. They're not just a critique — they're a roadmap.

## A practical first session

Spend 20 minutes doing this:

1. Open **All Jobs**, set the location filter to your city, scroll through the first few pages just to see what's out there. Do not apply to anything.
2. Open **Auto Match**. Look at the top 10 matches. Read the JDs. Bookmark anything that looks interesting (Chapter 10 covers Favourites).
3. Open **Manual Search**. Run one search with a salary floor. Compare the results to Auto Match.
4. Pick the mode that surfaced the best results for your situation. That's your default for next time.

## Checklist

- [ ] Tried All Jobs at least once
- [ ] Tried Auto Match at least once
- [ ] Tried Manual Search at least once
- [ ] Found at least 5 jobs that look genuinely worth applying to
- [ ] You understand what the AI Match Score and Ghost Risk Score mean
- [ ] You know which mode is going to be your default
