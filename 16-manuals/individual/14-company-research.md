# Chapter 14 — Researching a company before an interview

The single most reliable signal that someone is going to be a strong candidate is that they did the work to understand the company before the interview. It's also one of the easiest ways to pull ahead — most people skip it.

Sebenza Hub's **Company Research** tool (`/dashboard/individual/company-research`) makes the work shorter. Use it.

## When to do company research

There are two distinct moments:

| Moment | Purpose | Time investment |
|--------|---------|-----------------|
| **Before applying** | Filter out bad fits early so you don't waste effort | 5–10 minutes |
| **Before interviewing** | Build a rich understanding so you can have a real conversation | 20–45 minutes |

These are very different sessions. The first is triage. The second is preparation. Don't conflate them.

## Pre-application research (5–10 minutes)

Before you even add a job to favourites, glance at the company. You're looking for red flags or clear deal-breakers, not full understanding.

Open **Company Research** and search for the employer. The platform pulls together:

- **Basics** — name, size, industry, headquarters, founding year
- **Culture signals** — reviews, ratings, employee sentiment
- **Growth signals** — recent funding, hiring pace, expansion news
- **B-BBEE level** (where known) — relevant in South Africa
- **Recent news and announcements**

Things to look for in 5 minutes:

- **Are reviews catastrophically bad?** Universally bad reviews (everyone says the same negative thing) is a real signal. Mixed reviews are normal — every company has unhappy ex-employees.
- **Is the company actually hiring or shrinking?** A company that just laid off 30% of its staff is probably a bad bet, even if the listing is real.
- **Does the company exist?** Some aggregated job listings are from companies that have dissolved, rebranded, or changed names. Verify.
- **Does their industry, mission, or values match anything you actually care about?** If you can't find a single reason to want this job, that's a signal.

If anything is strongly negative, deprioritise the application — favourite something else instead.

## Pre-interview research (20–45 minutes)

Once you have an interview booked, do real research. Block 30 minutes and do it properly. This is the difference between an interview where you're guessing what they care about and one where you can have a real conversation.

### Step 1 — Read the basics again

Re-open Company Research for the company. Read everything the platform pulled in. Take notes (use Application Notes from Chapter 12 — they belong on the application record).

### Step 2 — Read the original job description carefully

You probably skimmed it when you applied. Read it now, line by line. Look for:

- **The hard requirements** vs the nice-to-haves
- **The "you should be" descriptors** — these tell you what they value in a person
- **Any unusual phrases** — "fast-paced", "cross-functional", "wear multiple hats" — each of these signals something specific about how they work
- **The team structure**, if mentioned
- **The reporting line**, if mentioned

Notes from this read are gold for your answers. If the JD says "we move fast and don't wait for perfect", you need a story about a time you did that.

### Step 3 — Look up the people

If you know the names of the interviewer(s) — and you usually do once the interview is scheduled — look them up:

- Their role at the company
- How long they've been there
- What they did before (often more revealing than what they do now)
- Anything they've published (blog posts, talks, LinkedIn posts)

You're not building a dossier. You're building enough context to ask one good question and to read the room.

### Step 4 — Recent news and momentum

Check what the company has been doing in the last 3–6 months:

- Product launches
- Funding rounds (if it's a startup or scale-up)
- Leadership changes
- Press coverage (positive or negative)
- Earnings (if it's a listed company)

You're looking for things you can naturally weave into the conversation that show you've been paying attention.

### Step 5 — Build two questions

The single most important output of pre-interview research is **two genuine questions** to ask at the end of the interview. Most candidates either have no questions (signals disinterest) or ask generic questions ("what's the culture like?") that signal they didn't prepare.

Good questions are specific to the company:

- "I read that you launched [specific product] in Q3 — how is the team handling the support load that came with it?"
- "Your CTO posted recently about migrating the platform to [technology]. Where is that work right now, and is the role I'm interviewing for involved?"
- "I noticed you went through a leadership change earlier this year. How has that affected the team you're hiring into?"

These questions:

- Prove you researched
- Show you're already thinking about the role in context, not as an abstract job
- Get you genuinely useful information about whether you want the job

Write the two questions down before the interview. Memorise them. Use them.

## Using research during the interview

You don't need to drop research bombs to prove you prepared. Far better to weave it in naturally:

- When asked "why are you interested in us?", reference one specific thing you learned, not a generic compliment.
- When asked about a strength, link it to something you know they value from the JD.
- When asked your own questions at the end, ask the two specific ones you prepared.

Don't overdo it. One or two well-placed signals are enough. Ten signals come across as showing off.

## What to do if the company has almost no public information

Some companies — especially smaller South African businesses — have almost no online presence. In that case:

- Lean harder on the JD itself
- Look for the company on LinkedIn (if they're there at all)
- Look up the interviewer individually
- Be honest in the interview: "I tried to research more about [company] but couldn't find much — what would you want me to know about you?" That's a good question, not a failure.

## Connecting research to the rest of your prep

Company research feeds back into the Interview Simulator (Chapter 13). Once you have the company context, run a final mock interview *with that context loaded* — pretending you're interviewing for *this company*, not a generic role. The simulator's questions get sharper, your answers get more specific, and the practice transfers more directly.

## Checklist

- [ ] Ran Company Research on at least one upcoming interview
- [ ] Re-read the original JD carefully
- [ ] Looked up at least one of the interviewers
- [ ] Wrote down at least two specific questions to ask
- [ ] Updated Application Notes with what you learned
- [ ] Did a final Interview Simulator session with the company context
