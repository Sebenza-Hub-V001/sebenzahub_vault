# Chapter 11 — Applying to a job: walkthrough

This is the chapter where you actually apply. By now your profile is solid, your CV has been reviewed, and you've found a few jobs worth your attention. Time to send the first application.

This walkthrough takes you through one application end-to-end and explains what each choice does. You'll do it slowly the first time and quickly after that.

## Before you click Apply

Open the job in front of you and ask three questions:

1. **Is this real?** Check the ghost-risk score (Chapter 9). If it's above 50, look the employer up separately before applying.
2. **Is the match score reasonable?** If it's below 40, decide whether you have a specific reason to apply anyway. Be honest with yourself.
3. **Do I actually want this?** Read the JD properly. Not every "interesting" job is worth a custom application.

If you can't answer yes to all three, close the tab. Your time is better spent on a job that survives the test.

## The application form

Click **Apply** on the job listing. The form opens. Most jobs ask for the same set of things:

| Field | Required | Notes |
|-------|----------|-------|
| **CV (resume)** | Yes | You pick from the CVs in My CVs |
| **Cover letter** | Often optional, sometimes required | More on this below |
| **Screening questions** | Sometimes | Short questions specific to the role |
| **Video introduction** | Rarely | If the role asks for one |
| **Documents** | Sometimes | Additional files (portfolio, references, transcripts) |

The exact set depends on what the employer or recruiter has configured.

## Step 1 — Choose the right CV

If you have multiple CVs from Chapter 5, pick the one most relevant to *this* role. Don't default to your standard CV out of habit if you have a better-tailored version.

The CV you pick is what the employer downloads. It's also what the AI re-reads to recalculate the match score for *this specific application*. A better-tailored CV means a higher final match score, which can affect your position in the recruiter's review queue.

> **Tailoring tip:** If you find yourself wanting to edit your CV for this one role, save the edit as a *new* CV in My CVs (e.g. "Senior PM — Acme Corp") instead of overwriting your existing version. You'll thank yourself the next time you apply to a similar role.

## Step 2 — Write (or skip) the cover letter

Cover letters split into three categories:

### When to write a real cover letter

- The job is a stretch and you need to explain why you're a fit despite the gap
- The role is at a company you really want to work for
- The JD specifically asks for one
- The role is senior enough that recruiters expect one

### When to skip the cover letter

- The form makes it optional and the role is high-volume / entry-level
- You have no specific angle and would just be padding
- The application is for an aggregated job where the cover letter may not even reach the employer

A bad cover letter hurts more than no cover letter. If you don't have anything specific to say, leaving it blank is better than writing generic filler.

### Using the AI cover letter generator

Sebenza Hub has a **Cover Letter** tool (`/dashboard/individual/cover-letter`) that drafts one for you based on your profile and the job description. It's useful, but with caveats:

- Treat the output as a **first draft, not a final version**. Read it and rewrite anything that sounds generic.
- The AI doesn't know your real motivation. The "why this company" line will always be the weakest part — overwrite it with something true.
- A two-paragraph cover letter that mentions one specific thing about the role beats a three-paragraph AI letter that could apply to any company.

> **Open question:** The Cover Letter tool may consume credits or require a paid plan. See [[08-questions/subscription-plans]].

## Step 3 — Answer screening questions

Some jobs include screening questions. They might be:

- Yes/no: "Do you have a valid driver's licence?", "Are you legally entitled to work in South Africa?"
- Short text: "Why are you interested in this role?", "Describe a challenging project you led."
- Longer text: "How would you approach the first 90 days in this role?"

A few rules:

- **Answer yes/no questions truthfully.** They are usually disqualifiers — lying gets you removed at the next stage.
- **Don't pad short text answers.** Two clear sentences beat a five-sentence ramble.
- **Treat longer text answers like mini-essays.** They are often the *only* part of your application a recruiter reads carefully. Spend the time.

## Step 4 — Optional: video introduction

Some roles ask for an application-specific video, separate from the Video Profile in Chapter 7. If so, the form will tell you the time limit (usually 60–90 seconds). Re-read Chapter 7's video advice — same rules apply.

If the form doesn't ask for one, don't volunteer it.

## Step 5 — Submit

Click **Submit** when you're done. The platform creates an application record with:

- **Status**: `Applied`
- **Pipeline stage**: `new`
- **AI match score** (recalculated against your chosen CV)
- **Matched skills** and **missing skills** lists
- **Ghost risk score** (if the platform detected anything)

You'll see a confirmation, and the application appears in My Applications immediately.

## What happens next (timeline expectations)

The platform doesn't control how fast employers respond. Realistically:

| What you might see | When |
|--------------------|------|
| Auto-acknowledgement of receipt | Within minutes (not always) |
| Status moves from `Applied` to `Screening` | Days to weeks |
| First message from a human | Days to weeks |
| Interview invitation | Days to a month |
| Rejection notification | Anywhere from days to never (some employers ghost) |
| No response at all | Common — a meaningful fraction of applications go silent |

If you don't hear back after 7–14 days, that's your cue to set a follow-up reminder (Chapter 12) — not to spiral.

## How to apply *well* — meta-rules

- **Apply to fewer jobs, better.** Five thoughtful applications outperform fifty rushed ones, every single time.
- **Apply in focused blocks.** As Chapter 10 suggested — favourite throughout the week, apply in one focused session.
- **Treat each application as a small project.** Re-read the JD. Customise the CV pick. Decide on the cover letter. Submit. Move on.
- **Track what you do.** Chapter 12 covers tracking; even simple notes ("Applied because of the remote setup, mention the SQL skill explicitly if interviewed") will help you when you actually get the interview.
- **Don't apply when tired or distracted.** A bad submission is hard to take back. Save the tab and come back fresh.

## What if you change your mind after applying?

You can withdraw an application at any time before the offer stage. Go to **My Applications**, open the application, and choose **Withdraw**. The status changes to `Withdrawn`. Withdrawing is fine — recruiters expect it occasionally and it doesn't damage your reputation on the platform.

## Checklist

- [ ] Submitted at least one application end-to-end
- [ ] Confirmed the application appears under My Applications
- [ ] Reviewed the AI match score and noticed any missing skills flagged
- [ ] You picked the right CV for the role rather than defaulting
- [ ] You decided consciously whether to write a cover letter (rather than just skipping by default)
- [ ] You know how to withdraw an application if you change your mind
